# English Legal Register Guide

## 1. Modal Verbs

| Verb | Legal Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| must | Obligation (mandatory) | "The Board must approve..." |
| shall | Obligation (traditional, declining use) | "The Company shall provide..." |
| may | Permission (discretionary) | "The Director may delegate..." |
| will | Future action / declaration | "The Company will indemnify..." |
| should | Recommendation (not binding) | "Parties should consider..." |
| is entitled to | Right | "The Employee is entitled to..." |
| must not / may not | Prohibition | "The Contractor must not assign..." |

**Preference**: "Must" over "shall" (per Bryan Garner). Use "shall" only if matching precedent or house style requires it. Never mix "shall" and "must" for obligations in the same document.

## 2. Legal Terms of Art

The following terms carry precise legal meaning and must be used accurately. Do not substitute synonyms.

| Term | Precise Meaning | Usage Note |
|---|---|---|
| material | Significant enough to affect a reasonable person's decision | Do not use as a general synonym for "important" |
| reasonable | Judged by the objective standard of a reasonable person | Context-dependent; often defined by case law |
| shall | Imposes a duty (traditional) | See Section 1 for modern alternatives |
| may | Grants permission or discretion | Never use to indicate possibility; use "might" or "could" for that |
| notwithstanding | Despite; regardless of; even if a contrary provision exists | Use to override a conflicting provision: "Notwithstanding Section 3.2, the Buyer may..." |
| pursuant to | Under the authority of; in accordance with | Acceptable in legal drafting; "under" is a plain-English alternative |
| provided that | On the condition that; but (introduces an exception) | Modern preference: replace with "but," "except," or "if" |
| including | Illustrative, not exhaustive (unless "including but not limited to" or "including without limitation" is specified) | Courts split on whether "including" is inherently non-exhaustive; add "but not limited to" for safety |
| represents and warrants | Two distinct legal concepts: representation (statement of fact) and warranty (guarantee of fact with remedy for breach) | Often paired as a doublet; some modern drafters separate them |
| indemnify | To compensate for loss or damage; to hold harmless | Distinct from "hold harmless" in some jurisdictions |
| terminate | To end a contract or obligation, typically prospectively | Distinguish from "rescind" (retroactive unwinding) |
| assign | To transfer rights under a contract to a third party | Distinguish from "delegate" (transfer of duties) |
| waive | To voluntarily relinquish a known right | Requires knowledge of the right being waived |
| estoppel | Prevention of a party from asserting a position inconsistent with prior conduct | A doctrine, not a drafting term — use precisely |
| force majeure | Unforeseeable event beyond the parties' control excusing performance | Define in the contract; do not rely on common law alone |
| pro rata | Proportionally | Specify the basis for proportionality |
| pari passu | On equal footing; ranking equally | Common in finance; specify what ranks equally |
| inter alia | Among other things | Prefer "among other things" in modern drafting |
| ab initio | From the beginning | Use when describing retroactive invalidity: "void ab initio" |
| bona fide | In good faith; genuine | Acceptable as term of art; otherwise use "good faith" |
| de minimis | Too small to merit legal attention | Acceptable as term of art |
| prima facie | On its face; sufficient to establish a fact unless rebutted | Acceptable as term of art |
| res judicata | A matter already judged; claim preclusion | Term of art in litigation |
| stare decisis | The doctrine of following precedent | Term of art in litigation |

## 3. Prohibited Patterns and Alternatives

| Prohibited | Correct |
|---|---|

| a lot of | numerous, substantial, significant |
| get, got | obtain, receive, acquire |

| it is important to note | (state the point directly) |
| I think / I believe | (state the conclusion directly, or "it is the author's opinion that" in memos) |
| pretty clear / really important | clear / important |
| in terms of | regarding, concerning, under |
| as per | under, in accordance with, per |

## 5. Formal Transition Catalog

### Addition
| Transition | Usage |
|---|---|
| Moreover | Adds a point of equal or greater weight |
| Furthermore | Adds supporting detail or a related point |
| In addition | Adds an additional point |
| Similarly | Draws a parallel |
| Likewise | Shows correspondence between points |
| Also | General addition (less formal; use sparingly) |
| Indeed | Strengthens the preceding point |
| In fact | Introduces a factual reinforcement |

### Contrast
| Transition | Usage |
|---|---|
| However | Introduces a contrasting or qualifying point |
| Nevertheless | Concedes a point but maintains the original position |
| Nonetheless | Same as "nevertheless" |
| By contrast | Highlights a difference between two things |
| Conversely | Introduces the opposite situation |
| On the other hand | Presents the alternative perspective |
| Yet | Introduces a surprising contrast |
| Although / Even though | Concessive clause introducing acknowledged counterpoint |
| Notwithstanding | Despite; regardless of (also a legal term of art) |
| Whereas | Introduces a contrasting fact (avoid in recitals; acceptable as contrast transition in briefs) |

### Cause and Effect
| Transition | Usage |
|---|---|
| Therefore | Conclusion follows logically from the preceding |
| Accordingly | Formal conclusion; consequence follows naturally |
| Thus | Conclusion drawn from evidence or reasoning |
| Consequently | Result or outcome of the preceding point |
| As a result | Outcome caused by the preceding |
| Hence | For this reason (slightly formal) |
| For this reason | Explicit causal link |
| Because / Since | Introduces the cause |
| It follows that | The logical next step |

### Example and Illustration
| Transition | Usage |
|---|---|
| For example | Introduces an illustrative instance |
| For instance | Same as "for example" |
| Specifically | Narrows from general to particular |
| In particular | Highlights a specific item |
| Notably | Draws attention to a significant detail |
| That is | Clarifies or restates |
| Namely | Identifies specific items in a list |

### Summary and Conclusion
| Transition | Usage |
|---|---|
| In sum | Summarizes the foregoing |
| In short | Brief restatement |
| To summarize | Announces a summary |
| Ultimately | Final conclusion after weighing competing considerations |
| In conclusion | Announces the final point (avoid in academic writing; acceptable in briefs) |
| On balance | After weighing both sides |

## 6. Active vs. Passive Voice

- **Prefer active**: "The Board approved the resolution."
- **Passive acceptable**: when the actor is unknown, unimportant, or when emphasizing the object: "The resolution was approved unanimously."
- **Strategic passive in briefs**: De-emphasize your client's unfavorable actions: "The payment was not made by the deadline" (passive) vs. "Our client failed to make the payment" (active — too direct when harmful).
- **Target**: At least 70% active voice in any legal document.

## 7. Tone Calibration by Document Type

| Document Type | Tone | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Office Memorandum | Objective, balanced, analytical | Present both sides fairly; use IRAC/CRAC; qualify conclusions ("likely," "probably"); address counterarguments honestly |
| Trial Brief / Motion | Persuasive, assertive, confident | Lead with strengths; minimize weaknesses without hiding them; use strong topic sentences; avoid hedging language |
| Appellate Brief | Formal, scholarly, persuasive | Deeper legal analysis; more citation to authority; respect the standard of review; measured tone |
| Court Filing (procedural) | Neutral, professional, concise | State facts and requested relief plainly; no advocacy beyond what is necessary |
| Corporate Resolution | Formal, declaratory, precise | "RESOLVED, THAT..."; recitals state background; operative provisions state action; no advocacy |
| Client Letter / Advisory | Clear, accessible, professional | Explain legal concepts in plain language; state conclusions clearly; outline risks; recommend action |
| Contract / Agreement | Precise, operational, neutral | No advocacy; define terms carefully; use "must" for obligations; anticipate disputes; aim for clarity over elegance |
| Demand Letter | Firm, professional, factual | State the claim, the facts, and the demand; avoid threats; set a deadline; maintain professionalism |
| Settlement Agreement | Neutral, precise, comprehensive | Both parties' obligations clearly stated; release language precise; no ambiguity about what is resolved |

## 8. Gender-Neutral Language Rules

### Mandatory Rules
1. **Never use "he" or "she" as a generic pronoun.** Do not default to masculine pronouns.
2. **Do not use "he/she" or "s/he."** These are awkward and disfavored.
3. **Singular "they" is acceptable** in modern legal drafting when referring to a generic individual, per the ABA and most modern style guides.

### Techniques

| Gendered | Gender-Neutral |
|---|---|
| "Each employee must submit his timesheet." | "Each employee must submit a timesheet." (repeat the noun or drop the pronoun) |
| "The director shall exercise his judgment." | "The director shall exercise the director's judgment." (repeat the noun — acceptable in contracts) |
| "When a party breaches, he is liable." | "When a party breaches, that party is liable." / "A breaching party is liable." |
| "chairman" | "chair" or "chairperson" |
| "policeman" / "fireman" | "police officer" / "firefighter" |
| "manpower" | "personnel" / "workforce" |
| "reasonable man" | "reasonable person" |
| "he or she" | "they" (singular) / rephrase to avoid pronoun |

### Preferred Approach (Ranked)
1. Restructure the sentence to avoid the pronoun entirely.
2. Use the plural ("employees... they").
3. Repeat the noun.
4. Use singular "they" (acceptable but some traditional readers resist it).

## 9. Numbers and Dates Formatting

### Numbers
| Rule | Example |
|---|---|
| Spell out zero through nine; use numerals for 10 and above | "three witnesses," "15 exhibits" |
| Spell out numbers that begin a sentence | "Forty-two days elapsed before..." |
| Use numerals for dollar amounts | "$5,000" not "five thousand dollars" (but in contracts, use both: "Five Thousand Dollars ($5,000)") |
| Use numerals for percentages | "15%" or "15 percent" (be consistent) |
| Use numerals for section/article references | "Section 3," "Article IV" |
| Use numerals for ages, dates, and addresses | "a 5-year term," "March 10, 2026," "123 Main Street" |
| In contracts, state amounts in words and numerals | "Thirty (30) days," "Five Hundred Thousand Dollars ($500,000)" |
| Use commas in numbers 1,000 and above | "1,000," "1,000,000" |
| Ordinals: spell out first through ninth | "the Third Circuit," "the 12th day" |

### Dates
| Format | Context |
|---|---|
| March 10, 2026 | Standard US legal format |
| the 10th day of March, 2026 | Formal/traditional (contracts, court filings) |
| 03/10/2026 | Avoid — ambiguous internationally |
| 2026-03-10 | ISO format — acceptable in technology contracts |

### Time
- Use "a.m." and "p.m." (lowercase, with periods).
- Use "12:00 noon" and "12:00 midnight" (not "12:00 a.m." or "12:00 p.m.," which are ambiguous).
- Specify the time zone: "5:00 p.m. Eastern Time."

## 10. Capitalization Rules for Legal Documents

### Always Capitalize
| Item | Example |
|---|---|
| Defined terms | "the Company," "the Agreement," "Closing Date" |
| Proper nouns | "United States," "State of New York" |
| Court names | "the Supreme Court," "the Southern District of New York" |
| Titles before names | "Judge Smith," "Chief Justice Roberts" |
| "Court" when referring to the court reading the document | "This Court should grant the motion." |
| Party designations when defined | "Plaintiff," "Defendant," "Buyer," "Seller" |
| Constitution and specific amendments | "the Constitution," "the First Amendment," "the Due Process Clause" |
| Specific acts and statutes by name | "the Securities Act," "the Clean Air Act" |
| Article, Section, Clause when referring to a specific provision | "Article III," "Section 2.1," "Clause 7" |

### Do Not Capitalize
| Item | Example |
|---|---|
| Generic references to courts | "the court below," "a federal court" |
| Generic legal concepts | "due process," "equal protection" (unless referring to a specific clause) |
| "plaintiff" / "defendant" when generic | "a plaintiff must prove..." |
| "section" when generic | "the above section," "several sections" |
| Seasons | "the spring 2026 term" |
| "federal" and "state" as adjectives (in most contexts) | "federal law," "state regulations" |

### Special Rules
- **"Party" references**: Capitalize when used as a defined shorthand for a specific party ("the Buyer," "the Lessee"). Do not capitalize when generic ("each party," "the parties").
- **"Board"**: Capitalize when referring to a specific, identified board of directors. Lowercase when generic.
- **Headings**: Use title case for headings in contracts and briefs. Use sentence case in memos if house style permits.

## 11. Hyphenation Rules

### Compound Modifiers Before a Noun
Hyphenate compound modifiers that precede the noun they modify:
- "a good-faith effort" (but "the effort was made in good faith")
- "a well-known rule" (but "the rule is well known")
- "a five-year term" (but "a term of five years")
- "a third-party beneficiary" (but "a beneficiary who is a third party")
- "a long-term agreement" (but "the agreement is long term")

### Do Not Hyphenate
- Adverb + adjective combinations where the adverb ends in "-ly": "a clearly erroneous standard" (no hyphen).
- After the noun: "The standard is clearly erroneous." (no hyphen)
- Proper nouns used as modifiers: "New York law" (no hyphen).
- Foreign phrases used as modifiers: "a bona fide purchaser," "an ab initio void contract" (no hyphen — the Latin phrase functions as a unit).

### Always Hyphenated (Legal Terms)
| Term | Note |
|---|---|
| arms-length / arm's-length | Hyphenated as modifier: "an arm's-length transaction" |
| cease-and-desist | "a cease-and-desist letter" |
| good-faith | As modifier: "a good-faith basis" |
| long-term / short-term | As modifiers before a noun |
| non- (prefix) | Generally hyphenated in legal writing: "non-compete," "non-disclosure," "non-binding" |
| pre- / post- | Hyphenated before proper nouns or dates: "pre-2020," "post-Civil War"; otherwise, check dictionary: "pretrial" (no hyphen), "pre-existing" (hyphenated in legal usage) |
| re- | Hyphenated when needed for clarity: "re-cover" (cover again) vs. "recover" (get back) |
| self- | Always hyphenated: "self-executing," "self-dealing" |
| co- | Varies: "co-defendant" (hyphenated), "cooperate" (no hyphen) |

## 12. Defined Terms
- Capitalize defined terms throughout: "the Company," "the Agreement."
- Define on first use with parenthetical: "XYZ Corporation (the 'Company')."
- Never vary the form of a defined term.
- If a term is used fewer than three times, consider whether it needs a definition.
- In contracts, consider a standalone definitions section (Article I or a Definitions exhibit) for terms used across multiple sections.
- In briefs and memos, define in the text on first use.
